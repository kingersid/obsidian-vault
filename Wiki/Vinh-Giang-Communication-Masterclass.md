## Core Framework #1 — The 5 Vocal Foundations

His single most-cited model. Each is a dial you can consciously turn:

1. **Rate of Speech** — modulate, don't stay constant. Fast = passion. Slow = profundity (even if the content is thin, slowing down makes it *feel* important).
2. **Volume** — signals confidence and authority. Most people under-shoot their natural volume by default and don't realize it; he gets students to record daily and review.
3. **Pitch & Melody** — most people speak in 2-3 notes. Widening your pitch range makes you more memorable and emotionally resonant — literally more "musical."
4. **Tonality** — map your voice to the 6 core human emotions (happy, sad, disgust, fear, angry, surprised). A monotone voice comes from a monotone *face* — body and voice are linked.
5. **The Pause** — most people fear silence as "looking uncertain." Reality: a pause gives your words weight and makes you sound more deliberate, not less confident.

**Actionable:** Pick ONE foundation per week. Record yourself daily on that single lever only (not all 5 at once) and review the footage — this is literally his coaching method with CEO clients.

## Core Framework #2 — Vocal Image vs. Visual Image

People spend disproportionate effort on visual image (posture, dress, grooming) and almost none on vocal image — even though voice is what converts an *assumption* ("they seem confident") into a *belief* ("they ARE confident") the moment you open your mouth.

**Actionable:** Audit your own "vocal image" the way you'd audit a LinkedIn photo. Record a 60-second intro of yourself and ask: does my voice match the impression I want my visual presence to create?

## Core Framework #3 — The 4 Communication Archetypes

From Stage Academy — useful for matching delivery style to context, not just content:

| Archetype         | Style                                              | Best for                                       |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| **The Motivator** | Higher volume, faster rate, expansive gestures     | Rallying a team, sales pitches, stage moments  |
| **The Educator**  | Slower rate, structured pauses, minimal movement   | Teaching, onboarding, tutorials                |
| **The Coach**     | Assertive, strong language, near-zero filler words | Feedback, accountability conversations         |
| **The Friend**    | Melodic tone, conversational rhythm, relaxed body  | Retention content, DMs, customer relationships |

**Actionable:** Tag your own content/conversations by which archetype you're *actually* using vs. which the moment calls for. A dealer acquisition WhatsApp voice note probably wants Coach or Motivator; a customer-retention reel probably wants Friend.

## Core Framework #4 — Storytelling Formula

Vinh's storytelling fix for flat, list-style retellings: most people give the "what happened" but skip the **who, stakes, and dialogue** that make a story sticky. His own example — turning "I met her at a bar, did some tricks, she didn't like it" into a scene with dialogue and tension — is the proof clip he uses constantly.

**Actionable rule:** Before telling a story (on camera or in conversation), identify:
- **Who** — give the other person a name/identity, not "someone"
- **Dialogue** — replace summary with actual lines said
- **Stakes** — what did you want, and what was the risk of not getting it

This maps directly onto [[Art-Of-Yapping-Masterclass]]'s "lead with the claim, cut the qualifiers" — Vinh's storytelling formula is the *long-form* cousin of the yapping hook: both work by cutting straight to the part that creates tension.

## Core Framework #5 — Accent & Confidence (his origin story, his most emotionally resonant content)

Vinh's own arc — bullied for his accent as a refugee kid, changed his name at 14, found confidence through magic before realizing voice itself was the real instrument — is the personal-story engine behind his most emotionally viral long-form content (Diary of a CEO episode, On Purpose episode). His teaching point: **accent has nothing to do with intelligence or competence; it's purely a habitual pattern, same as any other vocal habit, and is therefore just as trainable.**

**Actionable:** This is the model for Sid's own founder-story content — a specific, vulnerable, single-scene origin story (not a generic "started in 1985" heritage line) outperforms generic brand history every time. See Application section below.

## Recording & Delivery Tactics (Cross-Reference: Yapping)

Vinh's tactics overlap heavily with Oren John's Yapping doctrine but focus on the voice layer specifically:

- **Switch live between "wrong" and "right" delivery in the same sentence** — this is his single most repeated visual/audio device and the reason his clips get rewatched (compare to Yapping's "Ending Hook" — both exploit the brain's expectation-violation response)
- **Daily self-recording + review** — same idea as Yapping's "record in batches," but used for *skill-building* not just output
- **One lever at a time** — don't try to fix rate, volume, AND pitch simultaneously; isolate the variable, exactly like an iterative ad-testing approach
- **6 emotions mapped to face AND voice** — gives you literal building blocks for thumbnail/expression choice when storyboarding Reels
